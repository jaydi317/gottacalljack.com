Think Your Home Is Fine?
Check Again.

Most homeowners look at their house from the driveway and think, "Looks okay to me." But hail damage hides. Here's what it actually looks like up close — and why you can't see it from the ground.

🔍

Bruised Shingles

Hail creates soft spots where granules get knocked loose. The shingle looks fine from 30 feet away, but it's already compromised. Water will find its way in within months.

💧

Cracked Flashing

The metal around your chimney, vents, and skylights takes direct hits. Tiny cracks open up. These are the #1 source of "mystery leaks" that appear months after a storm.

🏚️

Dented Gutters & Downspouts

If your gutters have dents, your roof took the same beating — it's just harder to see. Dented gutters are the warning sign that roof damage is almost certainly hiding above.

🧱

Siding Impact Marks

Vinyl cracks. Aluminum dents. Wood splits. Damaged siding lets moisture behind your walls, leading to rot, mold, and insulation damage you won't discover until it's a major repair.

Here's what most people don't realize: A roof with hail damage can look completely normal for 6-12 months. Then the first hard rain or freeze-thaw cycle hits, and cracked shingles start letting water through. By then, you've got attic damage, wet insulation, and possibly mold. The repair that would have been covered by insurance at $3,000 is now a $10,000-$15,000 problem — and your window to file a claim may have closed.

Here's What Most People Don't Know

Your homeowner's insurance almost certainly covers hail damage. You're typically only responsible for your deductible. The insurance company pays for the rest — the roof repair, the siding replacement, all of it.

But here's the catch. Insurance companies don't call you. You have to file the claim. And you need a professional inspection report with photos to back it up. Without documentation, your claim gets denied or lowballed.
